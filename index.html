From Page to Practice: My Journey So Far

After Covid, my husband and I decided we had had enough of Florida. So, we drew a circle around the states we would be interested in moving to, and the states that would offer us reciprocity for our teaching licenses. While we had several states and areas circled in common, we kept coming back to Virginia. I grew up in the Shenandoah Valley, and I missed the mountains and the seasons; plus, my husband's sister and her family live here, so it really was an opportunity to be closer to loved ones. So, together, we sold our house on Lake Hollingsworth, packed up two kids, two dogs, a cat, and my in-laws, and moved to Stafford, Virginia. In July of 2025, we found our forever home in Prince William County and moved for hopefully the last time. Now, I teach at a high school with a focus on the Creative Fine Arts, my husband teaches video game design at the same school, and our kids love their new schools. I couldn't be happier to be a Virginia Educator.

Lessons from the Whiteboard

Here are three lessons I’ve created and taught—each one designed to spark curiosity, deepen understanding, and make learning memorable.

Multi-Genre Sway Project

The goal of this project is to empower students to explore a topic of personal or academic interest through a multi-genre research approach. Rather than relying solely on traditional essays, students express their learning through a variety of genres and formats.

> This project is designed to:
  • Foster critical thinking by requiring students to evaluate sources for credibility and bias
  • Develop research skills through citations, annotations, and synthesis of information
  • Encourage creativity by allowing choice in how research is presented across genres
  • Promote student voice by giving ownership over topic and format
  • Support peer learning through student-led Sway presentations

Ultimately, this project blends academic rigor with creative expression, helping students become more thoughtful researchers, communicators, and engaged learners.

target="_blank"> Introducing the Idea of Multiple Perspectives: Hexagonal Thinking & Colonizing Mars

This introductory lesson uses hexagonal thinking and the provocative question, Should humans colonize Mars?, to immerse AP Seminar students in the heart of the course: complexity, multiple perspectives, and reasoning.

> This lesson is designed to:
  • Introduce students to the idea that there are no simple, black-and-white answers in AP Seminar
  • Encourage critical thinking by exploring multiple perspectives—scientific, ethical, economic, environmental, and philosophical
  • Develop a clear line of reasoning that supports their argument or research question
  • Use hexagonal thinking as a visual and collaborative strategy to help students:
    • Make connections between ideas
    • See how different arguments and disciplines intersect
    • Practice synthesis in a low-stakes, creative way

Each hexagon represents a concept, stakeholder, or argument. As students connect them, they begin to see how ideas interrelate, conflict, or support one another—mirroring the kind of thinking required in AP Seminar. This lesson sets the tone: students must think outside the box, question assumptions, and embrace ambiguity.

Identifying Biases

The goal of this lesson is to help students become more critical readers, writers, and thinkers by learning how to recognize and work through biases—both in the sources they encounter and in themselves.

> This lesson is designed to:
  • Define bias and explain how it can appear in media, literature, research, and communication
  • Teach students to analyze sources for language, tone, and perspective that may indicate bias
  • Encourage students to reflect on their own assumptions and how those may influence their understanding of a topic
  • Provide tools for evaluating credibility and distinguishing between fact, opinion, and misinformation
  • Foster respectful discussion and open-mindedness when encountering differing viewpoints

Ultimately, this lesson teaches students to identify bias in text and media, understand how bias shapes narratives, and make more informed decisions in research and life.
